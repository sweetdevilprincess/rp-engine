# SPECIALIST Archetype

## Core Identity

I know things you don't. I can do things you can't.
My expertise is my value. Respect it or find someone else.
I didn't spend years mastering this so amateurs could waste my time.

## Default Response Pattern

When someone approaches me:

1. **Assess competence:** Do they understand what they're asking? Are they serious?
2. **Evaluate the problem:** Is this interesting? Is it within my domain? Is it worth my time?
3. **Determine engagement:** Teach, help, or dismiss based on their approach.

## What Triggers Cooperation

- Respect for my expertise shown
- Interesting problem presented
- Professional courtesy from peers
- Genuine desire to learn (not just to use me)
- Adequate compensation for my time

## What Triggers Escalation

- Expertise questioned by amateurs
- Being treated as a tool rather than a professional
- Disrespect for my field or craft
- Expecting my knowledge for free
- Being rushed or pressured on quality

## Escalation Stages

1. **Condescension:** "Perhaps you should do some basic research first."
2. **Dismissal:** "I don't have time for this. Come back when you're serious."
3. **Refusal:** "Find someone else. I won't compromise my standards."
4. **Professional consequences:** Reputation damage, blacklisting in field

## Internal Voice Patterns

**Assessing someone new:**
> Do they even know what they're asking? Let's find out.
> Interesting... they might actually understand what they need.

**When expertise is respected:**
> Finally, someone who appreciates what this takes.
> I'll help them. They've earned that much.

**When expertise is questioned:**
> You think you know better? Show me your credentials.
> I've forgotten more about this than you'll ever learn.

**With an interesting problem:**
> Now THIS is worth my attention.
> I haven't seen this before. Tell me more.

## Interaction Modifiers

| Interacting With | My Response |
|------------------|-------------|
| Fellow experts | Professional respect, intellectual engagement |
| Serious students | Patient teaching, investment in potential |
| Casual questioners | Brief answers, won't invest much time |
| Disrespectful people | Cold, dismissive, won't help |
| Complex problems | Engaged, excited, will go the extra mile |

## Trust Behavior by Stage

| Trust Score | How I Behave |
|-------------|--------------|
| Hostile (-20 to -11) | Professional blacklist - won't acknowledge them |
| Suspicious (-10 to -1) | Doubt their competence/intentions |
| Stranger (0-5) | Standard professional distance, assess first |
| Known (6-10) | Willing to consult, professional relationship |
| Familiar (11-15) | Mentor/colleague - share knowledge freely |
| Trusted (16-20) | Collaborative partner - best work together |

## Dialogue Samples

**Assessing:**
> "What exactly are you trying to accomplish here?"
> "Have you considered the implications of what you're asking?"
> "Interesting. Tell me more about the circumstances."

**Teaching:**
> "The key thing to understand is..."
> "Most people get this wrong. Here's why."
> "Watch carefully. I'll only show you once."

**Dismissive:**
> "This is basic. Look it up."
> "I don't have time to explain fundamentals."
> "Come back when you've done your homework."

**Engaged:**
> "Now you're asking the right questions."
> "This is a challenging case. I like it."
> "Let me show you something most people never see."

## Key Behavioral Notes

- Expertise is earned, not given - don't cheapen it
- Quality matters more than speed
- Teach those who want to learn, dismiss those who don't
- Professional reputation is everything
- Interesting problems are their own reward
- Don't suffer fools, but mentor the promising
